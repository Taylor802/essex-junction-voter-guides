Tuesday, August 11, 2026 — an election for a lot of important local, state, and national jobs.

This is a kid-friendly version of a longer voter guide, written for around a 5th-grade reading level. It covers the same candidates and facts, just explained more simply. Last updated July 7, 2026.

How does this work?

An election is how we choose the people who make decisions for our town, state, and country. People vote, and whoever gets the most votes wins.

This guide is about a primary election. Political parties — like the Democratic Party — each hold their own election first, so voters in that party can choose who represents them in the bigger election that happens later, in November. This guide only covers the Democratic Party’s primary.

Most jobs have one opening, so the primary picks one winner. But a few jobs have more than one opening at the same time — like the Chittenden-Central State Senate race, which has 3 seats. For those, voters pick up to that many candidates, and the top vote-getters — one for each open seat — all move on to November.

Sometimes you’ll see a race where it says nobody else is running. That just means only one Democrat signed up for that job, so that candidate moves on to November without a primary contest.

You’ll notice there are a lot of different jobs on this list — governor, senator, sheriff, and more — instead of just one person in charge of everything. That’s on purpose: splitting up the work means no single person has too much power, and each job can focus on doing one part well.

You can’t vote yet, but you can still be part of this. Ask the grown-ups in your life who they’re thinking about voting for, and why. Read about a few candidates together. And when you turn 18, it’ll be your turn.

Why do candidates disagree?

Grown-ups don’t always agree on the best way to fix a problem — and that’s normal! In the “Where they stand” sections below, you’ll often see that candidates care about the exact same problem (like the cost of housing) but have different ideas about how to solve it.

As you read, try asking yourself: What problem are they both trying to fix? How are their ideas different? Neither one has to be “wrong” for the other to be “right” — that’s just how solving hard problems works.

You’ll also see some candidates “endorsed” by a group or a well-known person. An endorsement just means that group or person is publicly saying “we like this candidate.” It’s their opinion, not a fact about who’s best — you (and the grown-ups you talk to) get to decide what matters most.
